Typical software company structure

XMind source file

CEO

  • Most senior manager who is responsible for everything
  • Always keep him/her informed about anything good and anything bad...
  • Unless it has an impact on your immediate boss
  • So it's better to consult with your boss before giving the feedback...
  • Though there are issues which may not be resolved on that level
  • Do not overflood CEO with your feedback; his/her time is usually the most expensive of all company members

Side departments

  • Do not blame them or quarrel with them in public, because you are not going to achieve anything but will make your life much harder
  • It can be a good strategy to get to know someone in sales or marketing and to promote your ideas directly through him/her
  • HR do hiring as well as deal with existing personnel. Example of cool HR. HR-s invaded LinkedIn and mine it, so you really should create a profile there.
  • Administrative department maintains internal company processes, policies; ensure cross-department communication; do many other useless and useful things.

R&D department

  • Treat the head the same as CEO
  • There can be no research actually; if it exists, try to get involved in it
  • There is a tendency to organize the work in an open space manner

Project Manager (PM)

  • Responsible for your project, communicates with the head of the department, CEO (less frequently) and the users (customers)
  • Delegates many tasks to others:
    • business and project analytics to the analysist (data scientist)
    • documenting to the technical writer
    • customer technical relations to the supporter
    • technical project details to team leads
  • Does the planning; controls the execution; solves non-technical problems
  • You are lucky if your PM is a solid professional; more likely he or she is a freshly promoted technical fellow (individual contributor) and has no idea about good project management and copies the bad practices - especially in Russia
  • If you want to become a good PM, study in Stratoplan or similar school.

Analysist

  • Responsible for requirements management
  • States particular business logic tasks
  • Analyzes how the project works to make the points of improvements
  • Communicates with customers
  • Does data mining; generates algorithms (less typical)

Technical writer

  • Responsible for documenting the project
  • Writes installation manuals, product usage manuals
  • Web projects often turn technical writers into copywriters; they compose the textual content
  • Can generate source code documentation (less typical)

Team lead

  • Usually, the most experienced developer who is responsible for the entire development branch
  • Plans and controlls during almost the same time as actually codes
  • Decomposes the high level tasks from PM and the analysist into smaller and more specific subtasks and distributes them among the developers
  • Codes the hardest and the most important parts of the project
  • Reviews code
  • Team leads can be young and dumb unexperienced - read about that problem. Do not be like them. Better to grow technically first

Architect

  • Technical guru who knows everything
  • Designs the project architecture, selects the best tools, language, etc.
  • Individual contributor who does not want to become a manager
  • Can specialize in different areas: DB, .NET, Java, Web, etc.

Developer

  • Junior - requires a supervisor
  • Middle - executes most of the tasks himself/herself
  • Senior - can execute the hardset tasks, evolves into a team lead or an architect

Testers

  • QA people help with finding and fixing bugs
  • Testing is often automated; the automation is coded by testers
  • Can confront with developers, because have different personal goals
  • Good testers are more rare to find than good developers (which are rare, too)

Designers

  • Imagine the site from a user's point of view
  • Create the media: images, videos, fonts
  • Create complete site layouts, usually in a layered graphics format; supply it to frontend programmers
  • Think about UX and take it into account

Development models: code&fix

  • There can be nothing wrong with it. See Programming, Motherfucker
  • Suitable for small (up to 7-8 devs) teams
  • Evolves to either Waterfall or Agile model - or the project terminates

Development models: Waterfall

  • Sequential processes (wiki)
  • Suitable for huge projects with high reliability of working units
  • Does not tolerate mistakes; they lead to code&fix

Development models: Agile

  • Iterative processes (wiki) which often use methods of extreme programming
  • The hardest model to follow but the most powerful
